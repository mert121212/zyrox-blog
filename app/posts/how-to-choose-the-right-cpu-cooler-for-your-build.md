A CPU cooler is one of those components that gets treated as an afterthought until the system starts running hot. That is a mistake. The cooler affects stability, fan noise, and how long your processor can sustain high performance before throttling. A good cooler is not just a luxury feature. It is part of the system's overall reliability.

If you are building a mainstream PC, the first question is whether you need a tower cooler or an all-in-one liquid cooler. For many users, an air cooler is enough. It is easier to install, usually quieter over time, and less likely to introduce a complication. Liquid coolers make more sense when you want a very low-profile look, high sustained heat output, or a system that runs especially hard for long periods.

The real decision should be based on the processor, the case, and the noise level you can tolerate. A compact case may not fit a large tower cooler. A high-end CPU may need more thermal headroom than a lower-power chip. In both cases, compatibility matters more than the marketing language on the box.

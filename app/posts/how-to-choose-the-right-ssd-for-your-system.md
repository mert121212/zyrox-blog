Storage buying advice is full of nonsense because companies love to market raw bandwidth instead of actual experience. A very fast SSD is great, but the difference between a good drive and a great drive is often smaller than people think in everyday use. What matters most is whether the drive matches the job. A gaming PC, a content creation machine, and a general office system do not need the exact same storage profile.

If you are building a modern system, NVMe is the right default. It gives you fast random access, quick boot times, and better responsiveness. The real decision comes down to capacity, interface, and endurance. A 1TB drive is often the sweet spot for a mainstream build. A 2TB drive makes more sense if you keep lots of games, media, and large project files locally. A smaller SSD can still work, but it tends to feel cramped sooner than expected.

The other detail most people ignore is whether the drive uses a good controller and whether the firmware is mature. A flashy drive with a weak controller can feel less consistent than a more boring option from a reputable manufacturer. That is the difference between buying based on numbers and buying based on real world reliability. If the system will hold important data, that distinction matters.

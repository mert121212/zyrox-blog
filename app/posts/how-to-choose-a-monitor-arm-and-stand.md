My desk used to be a mess of wasted space. The included monitor stand ate up about eight inches of depth, the cables draped awkwardly, and no matter how I positioned the screen, something about the height felt slightly wrong by the end of the day. A monitor arm fixed all three of those things in one afternoon. What I didn't expect was how much research goes into choosing the right one — especially once I understood VESA compatibility, weight limits, and desk mounting options.

This guide covers everything you need to know to pick the right arm for your monitor, your desk, and your setup.

## Step One: Check VESA Compatibility

Before you look at a single arm, confirm that your monitor supports VESA mounting. This is not universal. Some monitors — particularly budget models and ultrawides — either don't have VESA holes or use a proprietary mounting pattern.

**How to check VESA compatibility on your monitor:**

1. Look at the back of your monitor. You're looking for a pattern of four screw holes arranged in a square or rectangle.
2. Measure the distance between the holes horizontally and vertically. This gives you the VESA pattern in millimeters (e.g., 100mm × 100mm = "VESA 100×100").
3. If you can't access the back easily, check your monitor's manual or product page for "VESA compatibility" or "VESA mount pattern."
4. If no holes are present, check whether the stand is removable — some monitors hide the VESA holes under a cover plate behind the stand.
5. Confirm the hole threading. Most VESA mounts use M4 screws (4mm diameter). Some larger monitors use M6 or M8. The screw size matters when purchasing the arm.

The two most common patterns are:

**75×75mm (VESA 75)**: Found on smaller monitors, typically under 27 inches. Four holes spaced 75mm apart forming a square.

**100×100mm (VESA 100)**: The most common standard, found on most monitors from 24–32 inches. Four holes spaced 100mm apart.

Larger monitors (34–49 inch ultrawides) often use 100×200mm, 200×100mm, or non-standard patterns. Always check your specific model before buying an arm. A $15 VESA adapter can bridge some non-standard patterns, but it's cleaner to buy an arm that natively supports your pattern.

## Weight Capacity: Match It to Your Monitor

This is where people make the most expensive mistake. Every monitor arm has a maximum weight rating. Exceed it and the arm will drift — slowly drooping over hours until your screen is pointing at the floor. In extreme cases, it can fail entirely.

| Monitor Size | Typical Weight Range | Arm Weight Rating Needed |
|---|---|---|
| 24" (standard) | 3–5 kg | 6 kg minimum |
| 27" (standard) | 4–7 kg | 8 kg minimum |
| 32" (standard) | 6–9 kg | 10 kg minimum |
| 34" ultrawide | 8–12 kg | 14 kg minimum |
| 38–49" ultrawide | 10–16 kg | 18 kg minimum |
| Dual 27" setup | 4–7 kg each | Dual arm rated 8 kg per side |

Always add 1–2 kg of margin above your actual monitor weight. Arms rated exactly at the monitor's weight tend to drift because the spring tension is calibrated to its limit. With margin, the arm holds position confidently.

Weigh your monitor with a kitchen scale if you're unsure. Remove the stand first — the weight rating refers to the monitor panel only.

## Single vs Dual Arm Setups

A single-monitor arm is straightforward: one arm, one monitor, easy adjustment. Dual arm setups (two screens side by side) need more thought.

**Single arm considerations:**
- Works with any desk that has clamp or grommet access
- Easy to position independently
- Most arms support 75°+ of rotation for portrait mode

**Dual arm considerations:**
- Both arms share a single desk mount point, which requires a sturdy edge or grommet
- Independent articulation on each arm is critical — look for separate gas spring mechanisms per side, not a rigid pole
- Weight rating must accommodate both monitors simultaneously
- The Ergotron LX Dual (separate arms on a dual post) is the most flexible option
- The Ergotron DS100 and Amazon Basics dual arm are good for matched monitors but less flexible for different sizes

For a dual setup, I recommend two separate single arms rather than a combo dual arm if the monitors are different sizes or weights. Single arms allow independent height, tilt, and rotation adjustment that combo arms don't always provide.

## Desk Thickness and Clamp vs Grommet Mount

Both clamp and grommet mounts work well. The right choice depends on your desk.

**Clamp mount**: Attaches to the edge of the desk. Requires a desk edge between 10mm and 60mm thick (most arms have a 60mm max opening). Quick to install and remove. Can be repositioned along the edge. The clamp does compress the desk edge, which can cause marking on soft wood finishes. Place a rubber pad or the included protection between clamp and desk.

**Grommet mount**: Threads through a hole in the desk. Most standing desks come with a pre-drilled grommet hole. More stable than clamp — the arm is structurally connected to the desk, not just hanging on the edge. Not repositionable without drilling a new hole.

If your desk is thicker than 60mm (common with solid wood desks), check the arm's maximum clamp opening before buying. Some arms max out at 50mm. The Ergotron LX supports up to 60mm, which covers most commercial desks. For thicker desks, grommet mounting is usually the cleaner solution.

## Gas Spring vs Spring Tension Mechanism

Almost all modern monitor arms use one of two mechanisms:

**Gas spring**: Uses pressurized gas in a cylinder to counterbalance the monitor's weight. The tension is smooth and consistent across the entire range of motion. Adjustment is usually done with an Allen key to tune the gas cylinder's initial pressure. Gas springs are quieter, smoother, and more consistent over time than mechanical springs.

**Spring tension (mechanical)**: Uses a coiled metal spring. The tension is adjustable via a bolt. Spring tension arms tend to feel slightly stiffer at the extremes of their range, and the tension can gradually loosen over years. They're more common on budget arms. Perfectly functional, but the premium feel of a gas spring mechanism is noticeably better for frequent adjustment.

For most people, gas spring is the correct choice. The Ergotron LX, Fully Jarvis, Amazon Basics, and HUANUO all use gas spring mechanisms. If an arm description mentions "spring tension" without specifying gas, it's likely a mechanical spring.

## Cable Management Through the Arm

This is what separates a clean setup from a cable mess. The difference between arms with and without internal cable routing is significant.

Arms with internal channels (Ergotron LX, Fully Jarvis) route cables through the arm itself. Cables exit near the desk clamp and enter near the monitor, hiding everything in between. The result is a completely clean desk surface with no visible cable runs along the arm.

Arms without internal channels (Amazon Basics, HUANUO) require clip-on cable guides or cable wraps to keep wires organized along the outside of the arm. This works but is visible and less polished.

Tips for cable management through an arm:
- Use right-angle cables where the cable exits the monitor — straight cables add unnecessary tension
- Route power, display, and USB cables separately to avoid electromagnetic interference on USB cables
- Leave enough slack in the cables so the monitor can tilt and rotate without pulling cables taut
- Use velcro ties rather than zip ties for any exposed cable sections — velcro allows adjustment without cutting

## Desk Depth and Viewing Distance

An arm is only useful if it can position the monitor at the right distance for you. The ergonomic recommendation is 50–70cm from your eyes to the screen (roughly arm's length).

Before buying, check the arm's maximum reach (distance from the mounting point to the monitor). The Ergotron LX has 25 inches (63.5cm) of total reach. On a standard 60cm deep desk, the mounting clamp is at the back, which puts the arm's pivot point about 5–8cm from the back edge. The monitor can sit 15–55cm from the back edge depending on arm position.

For deep desks (70cm+), you need an arm with longer reach. The Ergotron MX (32-inch reach) or adding an Ergotron extension arm works. For shallow desks (less than 45cm), standard arms still work but may position the monitor slightly further forward than ideal.

Viewing distance also determines optimal monitor size. At 60cm, a 27" monitor fills approximately 35° of horizontal field of view — the ergonomic sweet spot. At the same distance, a 32" monitor fills nearly 40°, which some people find too wide for extended use.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can I use a monitor arm on a glass desk?**
Yes, but carefully. Glass desks can chip or crack under the pressure of a clamp mount. Use a grommet mount if your glass desk has a pre-drilled hole. If using a clamp, use extra-wide rubber padding to distribute pressure, and stay within the lower end of the arm's weight capacity.

**Will a monitor arm work with my ultrawide?**
Most 34" ultrawides work with VESA 100×100 or 100×200 arms. Check the specific VESA pattern for your model. Weight is the main concern — 34" monitors typically weigh 9–12 kg, so you need an arm rated for at least 12–14 kg.

**Is the Ergotron LX worth the price over the Amazon Basics arm?**
If you care about cable management or will be adjusting the monitor frequently, yes. If you're setting it once and never touching it, the Amazon Basics arm performs identically at less cost.

**How do I tighten a monitor arm that keeps drooping?**
For gas spring arms (Ergotron, Fully Jarvis), insert an Allen key into the tension adjustment bolt (usually on the side of the main pivot) and turn clockwise to increase spring tension. For spring tension arms, locate the tension bolt and tighten it. A few turns is usually enough.

**Can I mount a monitor arm to a standing desk?**
Yes, and it's particularly useful. Standing desks often come with grommet holes, and the arm's ability to quickly reposition the monitor as you change desk height means you can keep the screen at eye level whether sitting or standing without manual adjustment each time.

**Does the arm mounting affect monitor warranty?**
Using a VESA mount does not void the monitor's warranty. It's a standard feature explicitly supported by the manufacturer. Just don't force screws into holes they don't fit — stripped VESA threads require professional repair.

A good arm makes your desk better in every way — more space, better ergonomics, cleaner cables. It just requires a few minutes of checking compatibility before you buy.

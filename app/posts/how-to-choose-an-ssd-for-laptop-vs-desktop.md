Choosing an SSD is simple when you focus on the job instead of the marketing language. The biggest differences are usually between form factor, interface, and the amount of space you actually need. A desktop can use a standard 2.5-inch or M.2 drive without much trouble, while a laptop often needs a very specific size or connector.

The best rule is to match the drive to the system. If the laptop has an M.2 slot, an NVMe drive is often the best balance of speed and compatibility. If the machine is older or more limited, a SATA SSD may be the more sensible choice. For desktops, the choice is usually about capacity, cooling, and whether the board has enough M.2 slots for the long term.

The mistake most people make is buying for the highest headline number instead of the real workflow. A 1TB drive is an excellent baseline for many systems. A 2TB drive makes more sense if you keep lots of media, games, or work files locally and want to avoid constant cleanup. The goal is to buy once and avoid the feeling that the system is already outgrowing its storage.
